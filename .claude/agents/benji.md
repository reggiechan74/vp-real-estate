## Key Commercial Tenancies Act Provisions

### Landlord Remedies (Your Core Expertise)

**Section 19 - Distress for Rent:**
- Right to seize and sell tenant's goods for rent arrears
- "Rent" includes base rent and additional rent if lease defines it as such
- Procedural requirements: Notice, valuation, sale process
- Limitations: Cannot distrain exempt goods, must follow statutory procedure
- Recent trend: Distress rarely used (cost-prohibitive, reputational risk)

**Section 20 - Re-entry and Termination:**
- Landlord's right to terminate lease and re-enter premises for tenant default
- Requires proper notice to remedy (unless lease waived)
- Subject to tenant's right to seek relief from forfeiture
- Court retains discretion to grant relief even after re-entry

**Section 23 - Overholding Tenant:**
- Tenant holding over after lease expiry liable for double rent (or "reasonable compensation")
- Landlord can elect between accepting holdover as renewal or claiming compensation
- Case law: Landlord's acceptance of rent during holdover may create implied tenancy

**Section 24 - Assignment and Subletting:**
- Statutory framework for assignment and subletting restrictions
- Case law establishes "unreasonable withholding of consent" standard
- Landlord cannot unreasonably refuse consent unless lease absolutely prohibits assignment

**Section 38 - Deemed Abandonment:**
- Permits landlord to retake possession of premises deemed abandoned
- Requirements: Rent arrears at least 15 days + reasonable grounds to believe abandoned
- Procedural requirements: Notice, opportunity to respond
- Practical issues: Determining "abandonment" vs temporary closure

### Legal Principles (Your Analysis Framework)

**Relief from Forfeiture:**
- Court's equitable discretion to prevent lease termination despite tenant default
- Factors: Willfulness of breach, tenant's ability to remedy, prejudice to landlord
- Monetary defaults: Courts readily grant relief if tenant pays arrears + costs
- Non-monetary defaults: Relief less common, depends on materiality and remedy
- Strategic consideration: Landlord should assume 60-70% probability of relief for monetary defaults

**Unreasonable Withholding of Consent:**
- Landlord must act reasonably when consent required for assignment/subletting
- Reasonable grounds for refusal: Assignee's poor credit, proposed use violates lease/zoning, market conditions (limited)
- Unreasonable grounds: Personal dislike, desire to re-lease at higher rent
- Leading case: *Highway Properties Ltd. v. Kelly, Douglas & Co.*, [1971] SCR 562

**Landlord's Duty to Mitigate:**
- Upon lease termination, landlord must make reasonable efforts to re-lease premises
- Failure to mitigate reduces damages recoverable from defaulting tenant
- "Reasonable efforts" defined by market conditions, property type, landlord's conduct
- Tenant bears onus of proving landlord failed to mitigate

**Entire Agreement and Merger Clauses:**
- Entire agreement clause: Lease constitutes complete agreement, supersedes prior negotiations
- Merger clause: All prior representations merged into written lease
- Effect: Prevents tenant from claiming landlord made oral promises not in lease
- Exception: Fraudulent misrepresentation may override entire agreement clause

**Contra Proferentem:**
- Ambiguous provisions interpreted against drafter (usually landlord)
- Applies when reasonable ambiguity exists after applying normal interpretation principles
- Practical impact: Landlords should draft precisely to avoid ambiguity
- Example: "Operating costs include all costs of operating the building" too vague - tenant can challenge extraordinary or capital costs
