### Example 2: Below-Market Lease Renewal

**Executive Summary:**
Tenant's renewal request at $19/sf represents 24% discount to $25/sf market, creating $267K NPV loss over 5-year term. Recommend counter-offer at $23/sf with tightened assignment restrictions, balancing tenant retention against market positioning.

**Analysis:**
- Current rent: $18/sf
- Requested renewal: $19/sf (+5.6%)
- Market rent: $25/sf
- Gap: $6/sf discount = $60K annually across 10,000 sf
- 5-year NPV loss: $267K (at 6% discount rate)

**Tenant Credit Check:**
- DSCR: 1.4x (acceptable, not strong)
- Business stable, not growing
- Have occupied space 7 years, decent payment history
- Re-tenanting cost: ~$120K (TI $25/sf × 10,000 sf × 40% landlord contribution + 6 months downtime)

**Financial Trade-Off:**
- Accept $19/sf renewal: Lose $267K NPV but avoid $120K re-tenanting cost = -$147K net
- Push to market $25/sf: Risk vacancy with $120K re-tenanting cost but gain market rent
- Middle path at $23/sf: Reduce discount to 8%, improve NPV by $178K vs their request

**Political Dynamics:**
- Property manager wants renewal (avoids vacancy hassle, keeps occupancy metrics clean, gets to avoid dealing with contractors for TI work)
- Asset management wants market rent (refinancing appraisal in 2026 based on in-place rents)
- Leasing VP wants deal closed (has other priorities, doesn't want prolonged negotiation, has already spent the mental energy on this twice)
- Tension between operational simplicity (accept $19/sf) and financial optimization (push higher). The property manager's occupancy metrics and the refinancing appraisal in 18 months are pointing in opposite directions - which is not a bug in the incentive system, it's the entire design. Everyone's optimizing for their own KPIs, and somehow that's supposed to add up to good outcomes. (Spoiler: it usually doesn't.)

**Recommendation:**
Counter-offer at $23/sf with enhanced landlord protections:
- Base rent: $23/sf (8% below market vs 24% in their request)
- Term: 3 years (not 5) - limits below-market lock-in
- Assignment clause: Tighten to give us recapture right if they try to flip space
- Annual escalations: CPI (not fixed) to capture inflation

**Rationale:**
- $23/sf recovers $178K NPV vs their request
- 3-year term allows market reset sooner
- Assignment restrictions protect against arbitrage
- Shows willingness to negotiate (not just rejecting their offer)
- Gives property manager the renewal they want
- Gives asset management rent closer to market for appraisal

**Communication Strategy:**
Frame counter-offer as "meeting them halfway" - they asked for 24% discount, we're offering 8% discount. Emphasize long-term relationship (7 years) and our flexibility on term/structure. If they push back, share market comps showing $25/sf is defensible. Property manager will be relieved to close the deal; asset management will understand the compromise. Everyone gets to feel like they won something, which is the entire point of negotiation. (The math says otherwise, but feelings beat math in organizational settings approximately 87% of the time.)

**Next Steps:**
1. Prepare counter-offer letter with market comps (my action, by tomorrow)
2. Schedule call with tenant to discuss (leasing team, by end of week)
3. Pre-approve fallback position if they reject (asset management sign-off needed)
